Abstract
Semi-supervised learning (SSL) addresses the challenge of limited labeled data by leveraging abundant unlabeled data during model training. Among various SSL paradigms, pseudo-labeling has emerged as a particularly prominent and effective technique. This approach involves iteratively assigning artificial labels, or pseudo-labels, to unlabeled samples based on a model's high-confidence predictions, thereby expanding the effective training set. This survey paper provides a formal overview of recent advancements in semi-supervised learning utilizing pseudo-labels. We delineate the fundamental methodology, discuss key variations and enhancements, and analyze experimental observations from contemporary research. Special attention is given to techniques that improve pseudo-label quality, such as adaptive thresholding and the integration of consistency regularization. The paper concludes with a discussion of current challenges and identifies promising avenues for future research in this dynamic field.

1. Introduction
The escalating demand for high-performance machine learning models in diverse applications often confronts a significant bottleneck: the scarcity of expertly labeled data. Manual annotation is frequently labor-intensive, costly, and requires domain-specific expertise, making it impractical to scale for many real-world scenarios. Conversely, raw, unlabeled data is typically available in vast quantities. Semi-supervised learning (SSL) paradigms are designed to bridge this gap, enabling models to learn effectively from a small labeled dataset augmented by a large unlabeled corpus. By judiciously harnessing information inherent in unlabeled data, SSL methods aim to improve model generalization capabilities beyond what is achievable with purely supervised learning.

Among the various strategies employed in SSL, pseudo-labeling stands out for its conceptual simplicity and demonstrated efficacy across a wide range of tasks, particularly in deep learning contexts. The core idea behind pseudo-labeling, often referred to as self-training, involves an iterative process: a model is initially trained on the available labeled data, then used to predict labels for the unlabeled data. High-confidence predictions are subsequently treated as "pseudo-labels," and the unlabeled samples, now equipped with these artificial labels, are incorporated into the training process alongside the original labeled data. This process can be repeated or integrated into a single unified training objective. The appeal of pseudo-labeling lies in its ability to leverage the model's own understanding of the data distribution to progressively expand its knowledge without requiring additional manual annotation. This paper provides a comprehensive survey of the recent landscape of semi-supervised learning methods that rely on pseudo-label generation, examining their methodologies, experimental validations, and implications for future research.

2. Related Work
The concept of self-training, from which modern pseudo-labeling techniques originate, has a long history in machine learning. Early approaches often involved training a base classifier on labeled data, using it to label unlabeled instances with high confidence, and then retraining or augmenting the dataset with these pseudo-labeled samples [1]. With the advent of deep learning, pseudo-labeling has experienced a resurgence, becoming a foundational component in many state-of-the-art SSL algorithms.

Modern pseudo-labeling techniques frequently integrate with consistency regularization, a complementary SSL paradigm that encourages a model to produce similar outputs for perturbed versions of the same input [5]. This integration often stabilizes the pseudo-label generation process by ensuring that the assigned labels are robust to minor transformations of the input data. For instance, methods like Interpolation Consistency Training (ICT) leverage consistency on interpolations of samples, effectively enhancing the feature space utilization [5].

Recent research has focused on refining the pseudo-label generation process to mitigate issues such as confirmation bias, where initial model errors can be propagated and amplified through iteratively generated incorrect pseudo-labels. Innovations include adaptive thresholding mechanisms, as seen in "FreeMatch" [2], which dynamically adjust the confidence threshold for accepting pseudo-labels based on training progress or class distribution. Other approaches conceptualize SSL more broadly, such as "Learning to Impute" [1], which frames semi-supervised learning as a problem of imputing missing labels, where pseudo-labels serve as the imputed values within a general probabilistic framework. Furthermore, the interactions between labeled and unlabeled data have been explored explicitly, with works like "InterLUDE" [3] investigating how these interactions can be optimized to enhance learning, moving beyond simple augmentation by pseudo-labels. Pseudo-labeling has also found application in specialized domains, such as medical image analysis, demonstrating its adaptability to complex data types and multi-modal settings, as evidenced by "Semi-Supervised Multimodal Multi-Instance Learning for Aortic Stenosis Diagnosis" [4]. These advancements collectively highlight the evolving sophistication of pseudo-labeling strategies in addressing the challenges of limited labeled data.

3. Methodology
The fundamental methodology of semi-supervised learning using pseudo-labels centers on an iterative or joint training process that leverages a model's own predictions to expand its training data. Typically, the process begins with an initial training phase where a deep learning model, such as a convolutional neural network, is optimized using a small set of meticulously labeled data. This initial training provides the model with a rudimentary understanding of the underlying data distribution and discriminative features.

Following or in conjunction with this initial training, the trained model is then deployed to infer labels for a large pool of unlabeled data. For each unlabeled instance, the model outputs a probability distribution over the classes. A critical step in pseudo-labeling is the selection of pseudo-labels. Conventionally, a confidence threshold is applied: if the maximum predicted probability for any class exceeds a predefined threshold (e.g., 0.95), that class label is assigned to the unlabeled instance as its pseudo-label. Samples that do not meet this confidence criterion are typically discarded or re-evaluated in subsequent iterations, preventing the introduction of low-quality or noisy pseudo-labels into the training process.

The generated pseudo-labeled instances are then combined with the original labeled dataset. The model is subsequently retrained or fine-tuned on this expanded dataset, treating the pseudo-labels as ground truth. This iterative refinement allows the model to learn from a larger effective dataset, potentially capturing broader patterns and improving its generalization capabilities.

Recent methodological advancements aim to enhance the quality and robustness of pseudo-labels. "FreeMatch" [2], for instance, introduces a self-adaptive thresholding mechanism that dynamically adjusts the confidence threshold throughout training. This contrasts with static thresholds, which may be too restrictive early in training or too permissive later on. Adaptive thresholds can respond to the model's evolving confidence and the characteristics of the data, thereby optimizing the balance between exploiting unlabeled data and avoiding confirmation bias.

Another significant methodological trend is the integration of consistency regularization with pseudo-labeling. Methods like "Interpolation Consistency Training" (ICT) [5], while primarily a consistency regularization technique, exemplify how inducing consistency can stabilize the learning process from unlabeled data. When combined with pseudo-labeling, consistency regularization ensures that the assigned pseudo-labels are robust to data augmentations or perturbations, further improving their reliability. For example, a model might be encouraged to produce the same pseudo-label for an image and its horizontally flipped version.

"Learning to Impute" [1] offers a broader framework, framing semi-supervised learning as a data imputation problem. In this perspective, pseudo-labels are viewed as inferred values for missing labels, allowing the application of general imputation techniques. This generalized view opens avenues for more sophisticated statistical or probabilistic approaches to pseudo-label generation beyond simple thresholding. Furthermore, approaches like "InterLUDE" [3] focus on optimizing the interactive relationship between labeled and unlabeled data, suggesting that the manner in which pseudo-labels are generated and utilized can be refined by considering the specific contributions of both data types. In specialized applications, such as the "Semi-Supervised Multimodal Multi-Instance Learning for Aortic Stenosis Diagnosis" [4], pseudo-labeling is adapted to handle complex data structures, including multiple modalities and instances, demonstrating its versatility in real-world, intricate scenarios. These diverse methodologies collectively aim to improve the accuracy and stability of pseudo-label generation, making the overall SSL framework more effective.

5. Discussion
The widespread adoption and continuous evolution of pseudo-labeling in semi-supervised learning underscore its fundamental importance as a practical and effective strategy for leveraging unlabeled data. The core strength of pseudo-labeling lies in its intuitive appeal and ease of integration into existing deep learning pipelines, effectively transforming unlabeled examples into additional training signals. This simplicity has facilitated its application across diverse domains, from standard image classification tasks to complex medical diagnoses involving multimodal data.

One of the primary benefits observed in recent research is the significant improvement in model generalization capabilities, particularly when the amount of labeled data is severely limited. By iteratively expanding the training set with high-confidence pseudo-labeled examples, models can learn more robust representations and decision boundaries that are less prone to overfitting the small labeled subset. This is evident in the performance gains reported on benchmark datasets like CIFAR and ImageNet with methods employing refined pseudo-labeling strategies [2].

However, the efficacy of pseudo-labeling is not without challenges. A critical concern is confirmation bias, where initial errors or biases in the model's predictions on unlabeled data can be amplified as these incorrect pseudo-labels are fed back into the training process. This can lead to a degradation in performance, especially in scenarios with high noise or significant class imbalance. The development of adaptive thresholding mechanisms, as demonstrated by "FreeMatch" [2], represents a significant step towards mitigating this issue by dynamically adjusting the confidence requirements for pseudo-label acceptance, thereby reducing the propagation of erroneous labels and improving overall robustness.

Another crucial aspect of pseudo-labeling's success is its synergy with consistency regularization. While pseudo-labeling focuses on assigning discrete labels, consistency regularization encourages stable predictions under perturbations. When combined, consistency regularization can act as a crucial stabilizing force, ensuring that the pseudo-labels generated are not arbitrary but reflect the underlying manifold structure of the data more accurately. "Interpolation Consistency Training" [5] exemplifies how consistent predictions across various data transformations can lead to more reliable learning from unlabeled examples.

The "Learning to Impute" framework [1] offers an analytical perspective, conceptualizing pseudo-labeling as an imputation problem. This broader view allows for the application of more sophisticated statistical and machine learning techniques for estimating missing labels, potentially moving beyond simple confidence thresholding to incorporate probabilistic models of uncertainty. Furthermore, the focus on "Interactions between Labeled and Unlabeled Data" [3] suggests that future advancements might involve more nuanced strategies for how these two data types inform each other, rather than simply using pseudo-labels as a one-way bridge. This could involve co-training paradigms or more sophisticated feedback loops that enhance the quality of pseudo-labels through a deeper understanding of data relationships.

Finally, the successful application of pseudo-labeling in highly specialized contexts, such as "Semi-Supervised Multimodal Multi-Instance Learning for Aortic Stenosis Diagnosis" [4], highlights its versatility. This demonstrates that pseudo-labeling is not confined to standard image classification but can be adapted to complex data structures and critical real-world problems where expert annotations are exceedingly scarce and expensive. The ability to handle multimodal and multi-instance learning within an SSL framework using pseudo-labels is a testament to the technique's adaptability and potential.

6. Conclusion and Future Work
Semi-supervised learning using pseudo-labels has firmly established itself as a cornerstone methodology for effectively training robust models in data-scarce environments. This survey has explored the foundational principles of pseudo-label generation, detailing how models leverage their own high-confidence predictions on unlabeled data to augment the training set. We have highlighted significant advancements, including adaptive thresholding techniques that enhance the quality and reliability of pseudo-labels, and the crucial integration with consistency regularization methods that stabilize the learning process. The experimental observations from various studies consistently demonstrate the capacity of these methods to achieve substantial performance gains across diverse datasets and application domains, from general image recognition to specialized medical diagnostics.

Despite its demonstrated effectiveness, pseudo-labeling continues to present avenues for further research. The pervasive challenge of confirmation bias, where early model errors can be perpetuated through incorrect pseudo-labels, remains a critical area for innovation. Future work could focus on developing more sophisticated mechanisms for uncertainty quantification in pseudo-label assignment, moving beyond simple confidence scores to incorporate Bayesian approaches or ensemble methods that provide more reliable estimates of prediction certainty.

Another promising direction involves the exploration of more advanced feedback mechanisms between labeled and unlabeled data, as suggested by research into their interactions. This might include dynamic weighting schemes for pseudo-labeled samples based on their estimated quality or importance, or the development of co-training frameworks where multiple models collaboratively generate and refine pseudo-labels. Furthermore, the integration of pseudo-labeling with other emerging SSL paradigms, such as active learning or generative models, could yield more powerful and efficient learning systems. For instance, active learning could strategically select the most informative unlabeled samples for pseudo-labeling, while generative models could synthesize new, high-quality pseudo-labeled examples.

Finally, extending the applicability of pseudo-labeling to new data modalities and complex tasks, such as graph-structured data, time series analysis, or reinforcement learning, represents a fertile ground for future investigation. Addressing computational efficiency and scalability for very large datasets and models will also be crucial for the continued advancement and real-world impact of semi-supervised learning using pseudo-labels. The continuous refinement of pseudo-labeling strategies holds the promise of unlocking even greater potential from vast unlabeled data reserves, paving the way for more autonomous and less data-hungry artificial intelligence systems.
