Abstract
The rapid proliferation of Large Language Models (LLMs) has revolutionized various natural language processing tasks, including machine translation, social media analytics, and fact-checking. However, a significant and persistent challenge lies in detecting and mitigating biases embedded within these models, particularly in multilingual contexts. Multilingual LLMs often exhibit uneven performance across different language families and specialized domains, alongside encoding and amplifying various forms of bias present in their vast training data. These biases manifest as disparate performance in high- vs. low-resource languages, mispredictions in offensive language detection, reinforcement of social stereotypes, and skewed information retrieval. This survey synthesizes recent advancements in understanding and detecting these biases, examining novel frameworks, language-specific datasets, and evaluation methodologies. It critically analyzes experimental observations concerning language bias, retrieval bias, and annotation bias, offering a comprehensive overview of the current state of bias detection in multilingual LLMs and outlining critical areas for future research.

1. Introduction
The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has ushered in an era of unprecedented capabilities in natural language understanding and generation, fundamentally transforming fields such as machine translation, content moderation, and information retrieval [1]. These models demonstrate remarkable fluency and contextual awareness across hundreds of languages and diverse textual domains. Despite their transformative potential, a critical concern revolves around the inherent biases learned and propagated by LLMs from their training data [3]. In multilingual settings, these biases are particularly complex, often leading to uneven performance across languages, cultural insensitivity, and the amplification of societal stereotypes, posing significant challenges for fairness and equitable application [1], [5].

Bias in multilingual LLMs can manifest in various forms, including disparities in performance between high-resource and low-resource languages, gender or nationality stereotypes, and contextual or cultural misinterpretations [4], [5]. Detecting these biases is paramount to ensuring the responsible development and deployment of LLMs, especially given their increasing integration into sensitive applications. This survey paper aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the current research landscape concerning bias detection in multilingual LLMs. We synthesize existing methodologies, analyze experimental findings, and discuss the challenges and future directions in this critical area. The subsequent sections delve into related work, examine various detection methodologies, analyze experimental results, discuss overarching themes, and conclude with future research avenues.

2. Related Work
Research into bias in multilingual LLMs highlights its pervasive nature across various applications. Abdullah Sayeedi et al. [1] introduced "Translation Tangles," a unified framework and dataset specifically designed to evaluate translation quality and fairness in open-source LLMs. Their work underscored that LLMs exhibit uneven performance and can encode or amplify biases, particularly affecting low-resource languages, even in translation tasks. This observation aligns with findings from Vykopal et al. [4], who investigated language and retrieval bias in multilingual Previously Fact-Checked Claim Detection (PFCD). They found that multilingual LLMs often perform disproportionately better on high-resource languages like English compared to their low-resource counterparts, a phenomenon they termed "language bias." Furthermore, Vykopal et al. [4] introduced the concept of "retrieval bias," where information retrieval systems favor certain information, skewing the retrieval process itself.

Beyond performance disparities, LLMs also exhibit biases in sensitive content moderation and social representation. He et al. [2] evaluated LLMs on multilingual offensive language detection across English, Spanish, and German, demonstrating the models' potential but also highlighting inherent biases in both LLMs and datasets that contribute to mispredictions related to sensitive topics. Recognizing the language and culture-specific nature of bias, Grigoreva et al. [3] addressed a gap in evaluation by presenting RuBia, a bias detection dataset specifically for the Russian language. RuBia facilitates the diagnostic evaluation of state-of-the-art LLMs, revealing their predisposition to social biases across domains like gender, nationality, and socio-economic status.

A foundational challenge in addressing bias in multilingual LLMs stems from annotation processes. Cui et al. [5] proposed a comprehensive framework for understanding annotation bias in NLP datasets, distinguishing among instruction bias, annotator bias, and contextual and cultural bias. This framework is crucial for understanding how biases are introduced during dataset creation, directly impacting the fairness of multilingual LLMs. Together, these works collectively illustrate the multifaceted nature of bias in multilingual LLMs, ranging from performance gaps and specific task-related biases to the fundamental biases embedded in data annotation and retrieval mechanisms.

3. Methodology
Bias detection in multilingual LLMs employs a variety of methodologies, encompassing specialized datasets, evaluation metrics, and analytical frameworks tailored to capture diverse forms of bias. A common approach involves the creation of language and culture-specific datasets designed to expose stereotypical or unfair model behaviors. For instance, the RuBia dataset [3] was developed for the Russian language, containing nearly 2,000 unique sentence pairs across domains such as gender, nationality, and socio-economic status, with each pair consisting of a stereotype-reinforcing sentence and a contradicting one. Similarly, Abdullah Sayeedi et al. [1] introduced "Translation Tangles," a unified framework and dataset that includes a high-quality, bias-annotated dataset of 1,439 translation-reference pairs evaluated by humans. For evaluating previously fact-checked claim detection, the AMC-16K dataset was utilized to assess language and retrieval bias across 20 languages [4]. In offensive language detection, augmented translation data was employed to test LLMs in non-English contexts [2].

Beyond bespoke datasets, several studies integrate hybrid pipelines and comprehensive frameworks for bias detection. Abdullah Sayeedi et al. [1] proposed a hybrid bias detection pipeline that combines rule-based heuristics, semantic similarity filtering, and LLM-based validation to evaluate fairness. Cui et al. [5] developed a comprehensive framework specifically for understanding annotation bias, distinguishing between instruction, annotator, and contextual/cultural biases, which is vital for multilingual settings.

Evaluation metrics are crucial for quantifying bias. In translation tasks, metrics include traditional translation quality alongside specific fairness metrics [1]. For offensive language detection, performance metrics are directly applied to identify mispredictions related to sensitive topics [2]. The diagnostic evaluation of social biases typically involves analyzing model responses on stereotype-laden prompts [3]. In the context of fact-checking, both monolingual and cross-lingual performance are assessed, with retrieval bias being quantified by analyzing the frequency of retrieved claims by multilingual embedding models [4]. For annotation bias, detection methods include inter-annotator agreement, model disagreement, metadata analysis, multilingual model divergence, and cultural inference [5]. These diverse methodological approaches collectively contribute to a more nuanced understanding and measurement of bias in multilingual LLMs.

4. Experimental Analysis
Experimental investigations into bias detection in multilingual LLMs have yielded crucial insights into their performance disparities and inherent biases. Abdullah Sayeedi et al. [1] benchmarked open-source LLMs across 24 bidirectional language pairs and multiple domains using their "Translation Tangles" framework. Their findings consistently indicated that LLMs exhibit uneven performance across language families and domains, demonstrating a clear propensity to encode and amplify existing biases, particularly affecting low-resource languages. The hybrid bias detection pipeline employed in their study successfully identified these subtle and overt biases in translation outputs.

In the realm of offensive language detection, He et al. [2] conducted evaluations of GPT-3.5, Flan-T5, and Mistral across English, Spanish, and German in both monolingual and multilingual settings. Their experiments revealed that while LLMs possess encouraging potential for this task, the impact of different prompt languages and augmented translation data significantly influenced performance. Critically, their analysis highlighted that inherent biases within the LLMs themselves and their training datasets led to systematic mispredictions, especially concerning sensitive topics, underscoring the challenges of achieving unbiased content moderation.

Grigoreva et al. [3] utilized the newly introduced RuBia dataset to perform a diagnostic evaluation of state-of-the-art LLMs for Russian language bias detection. Their experiments showcased the models’ predisposition to social biases across domains such as gender, nationality, socio-economic status, and other diverse categories. This diagnostic approach effectively illustrated how LLMs perpetuate stereotypes specific to a given culture and language, thereby validating the utility of culturally specific datasets for bias assessment.

Vykopal et al. [4] investigated language and retrieval bias in multilingual Previously Fact-Checked Claim Detection (PFCD) using six open-source multilingual LLMs and the AMC-16K dataset across 20 languages. Their fully multilingual prompting strategy uncovered significant disparities in both monolingual and cross-lingual performance, unequivocally demonstrating persistent language bias where models performed better on high-resource languages. Furthermore, their analysis of retrieval bias, using multilingual embedding models, revealed that certain claims were retrieved disproportionately, leading to an inflated perception of performance for popular claims while under-representing less common ones.

Finally, while Cui et al. [5] presented a framework rather than specific experimental results with LLMs, their work synthesizes crucial detection methods for annotation bias. This includes inter-annotator agreement, model disagreement, metadata analysis, multilingual model divergence, and cultural inference. The implications of these detection methods are that biases often originate from the human annotation process or the contextual understanding embedded in data, which subsequently manifest as performance and fairness issues in multilingual LLMs.

5. Discussion
The collective findings from recent research underscore the pervasive and multifaceted nature of bias in multilingual Large Language Models. A recurring theme is the observation of uneven performance across languages and domains, with low-resource languages consistently demonstrating heightened vulnerability to bias amplification and performance degradation [1], [4]. This "language bias" significantly impedes the equitable deployment of LLMs globally, necessitating targeted interventions for less-represented linguistic communities.

Beyond language-specific disparities, social and cultural biases are deeply embedded within LLM outputs, as evidenced by evaluations in offensive language detection and diagnostic assessments using culture-specific datasets [2], [3]. These biases are often learned from the vast, yet inherently biased, pre-training data, which reflects societal stereotypes and historical inequalities. The introduction of datasets like RuBia [3] highlights the crucial need for language and culture-specific resources to effectively diagnose and understand these nuanced biases, as generic English-centric evaluations may fail to capture unique cultural phenomena.

A significant challenge, highlighted by the investigation into annotation bias [5], is that bias can be inadvertently introduced or exacerbated during the data annotation phase itself. Task framing, annotator subjectivity, and cultural mismatches among annotators contribute to "instruction bias," "annotator bias," and "contextual and cultural bias." This emphasizes that the fight against LLM bias must extend beyond model architectures to foundational data collection and curation practices. Furthermore, the concept of "retrieval bias" [4] introduces another layer of complexity, demonstrating that even the mechanisms for accessing information can be skewed, leading to disproportionate representation of certain claims and potentially influencing downstream tasks like fact-checking.

The variety of detection methodologies, from hybrid pipelines incorporating rule-based heuristics and semantic filtering [1] to comprehensive frameworks leveraging inter-annotator agreement and cultural inference [5], indicates a growing sophistication in identifying diverse bias types. However, a significant limitation across current approaches is the scarcity of standardized, comprehensive, and culturally validated multilingual datasets for bias evaluation. The reliance on domain-specific or language-specific datasets, while valuable, points to a broader need for unified benchmarks that can robustly assess fairness across a wide spectrum of linguistic and cultural contexts. Moreover, the dynamic nature of LLMs and the evolution of societal biases mean that detection methods must continuously adapt to remain effective.

6. Conclusion and Future Work
The survey of recent literature unequivocally establishes that bias detection in multilingual Large Language Models is a critical and complex endeavor. Current research demonstrates that LLMs exhibit significant performance gaps and actively encode and amplify various forms of bias, including language bias, social stereotypes, offensive content misclassification, and retrieval bias across diverse linguistic and cultural contexts [1], [2], [3], [4]. These biases are deeply rooted in training data, annotation processes, and the inherent structural tendencies of the models themselves [5]. While significant progress has been made in developing specific datasets and diagnostic frameworks, a universal and robust solution for detecting all forms of bias across the vast landscape of multilingual LLMs remains elusive.

Future work in bias detection for multilingual LLMs should prioritize several key areas. First, there is an urgent need for the development of more comprehensive, standardized, and culturally sensitive multilingual bias detection datasets that span a wider array of languages and domains than currently available [3]. These datasets should be meticulously crafted with diverse cultural perspectives to avoid introducing new annotation biases [5]. Second, research should focus on developing more sophisticated and adaptive bias detection frameworks that can operate effectively across different languages without requiring extensive language-specific adaptation. This might involve leveraging cross-lingual transfer learning techniques or developing universal bias indicators. Third, further investigation is warranted into the interplay between different types of biases, such as how language bias might exacerbate retrieval bias or how annotation bias propagates into social stereotypes during generation [4], [5]. Finally, a greater emphasis on proactive mitigation strategies during model pre-training and fine-tuning, particularly for low-resource languages, alongside robust post-deployment monitoring systems, will be essential for fostering more equitable and fair multilingual LLM applications.
